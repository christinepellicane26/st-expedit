Get to know St. Expedit

Patron saint for expedited help, and protection during travel

St. Expedit is the patron saint of urgent requests that need a quick resolution. Shrines dedicated to his likeness can be found tucked away along the roadsides of Reunion Island, where he is also asked for protection during travel.

Legend has it that nuns who first settled on the island requested some religious statuary from the Vatican. Boxes arrived marked "Expedit" (rush) and the Roman soldier statues contained within were thought to be the likeness of a saint named Expedit.

The story associated with this saint is one of a Roman soldier who wanted to become a Christian. The devil appeared to him as a crow saying, "Tomorrow! Tomorrow!" (the Latin word for tomorrow is cras,the sound that a crow makes.) He subdued the crow declaring, "I will be a Christian today!" He died soon after, forever exemplifying the need to stop procrastinating.

This is why he is depicted with a crow underfoot, and a cross in hand. The palm frond symbolizes martyrdom, and the cross has "Hodie" (Latin for "today") written on it. Apparently, there were also images of St. Expedit holding a clock instead of a cross. This is the Expedit depicted here, as he is not officially recognized by the Catholic church, and is not openly invoked by Christians (hence the reason so many Christians tend to visit his shrines in secret, and thank him privately.)

While he has become the "go-to" saint for nearly all requests, this incarnation of him focuses on the need for very fast resolutions to problems, and protection during travel. Instead of a cross, he his holding a cog, to symbolize both smooth travel and the passage of time (as inside a clock.)

The palm frond is paired with a water bottle, important for sustaining the well-being of travelers, especially those traveling under their own power, as Roman soldiers often did. His helmet symbolizes protection, especially important for cyclists.

His shrines are painted red and hidden away, usually in neutral territory, such as roadsides.

He accepts gold-colored tokens of appreciation (bright copper pennies are close enough!) which symbolize the payment Roman soldiers would normally receive. Because the soldiers traveled under their own power, very often walking long distances, St. Expedit accepts gold-colored food as well, pound cake being the usual offering.

Sometimes, people will behead the statues in an effort to break a curse, or in anger for a request not met. However, St. Expedit is a benign entity, and is not to be petitioned for malicious purposes. Curses are not to be inflicted in his name; his purpose is to serve and protect.

Those who have paid their respects to this incarnation of St. Expedit have been pleasantly surprised by how quickly and expeditiously their requests have been granted, and how efficient their traveling has been.

This is my personal tribute to the saint, whom I first learned about during a trip to Reunion Island several years ago. This is why I use the anglicized-French spelling of the name.
